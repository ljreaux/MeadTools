# Onion Mead Pan Sauce Test – Procedure

## Goal

Evaluate the flavor impact of three different meads when used to build a chicken pan sauce:

1. Traditional mead (control – onion added during sauce)
2. Onion mead
3. Caramelized onion mead

Each sauce will be prepared using the same process and paired with one chicken thigh. Differences in flavor should primarily come from the mead used.

---

## Materials

### Proteins

- 3 chicken thighs (one per sauce)

### Meads

- 100 g Traditional mead (control)
- 100 g Onion mead
- 100 g Caramelized onion mead

### Other Ingredients

- 72 g diced onion (for control sauce only – matches 6 lb/gal onion concentration)
- 3 demi‑glace cubes (1 per sauce)
- 30 g cold butter (10 g per sauce, for mounting)
- Neutral cooking oil
- Salt

### Equipment

- Large skillet or sauté pan
- Knife and cutting board
- Fine mesh strainer
- Spoon or spatula
- Small containers for tasting

---

## Sauce Assignments

| Sauce | Mead Used              | Onion Added During Cooking | Strain Sauce |
| ----- | ---------------------- | -------------------------- | ------------ |
| A     | Traditional Mead       | Yes                        | Yes          |
| B     | Onion Mead             | No                         | No           |
| C     | Caramelized Onion Mead | No                         | No           |

Notes:

- Sauce A serves as the control and will simulate onion flavor added during cooking.
- Sauce B and C rely entirely on the onion character already present in the mead.

---

## Preparation

1. Finely dice **72 g onion** for the control sauce.
2. Weigh **100 g of each mead** into three separate containers.
3. Season chicken thighs evenly with **~2% salt**.
4. Set aside **1 demi‑glace cube and 10g butter per sauce**.

Reference for onion calculation:

The onion meads were made with **6 lb onion per gallon of mead**.

This equals approximately:

```
~0.72 g onion per mL mead
```

For this test:

```
100 g mead × 0.72 = ~72 g onion
```

---

## Standardization Notes

To keep the comparison fair:

- Use the **same weight of mead** for each sauce.
- Use **1 demi‑glace cube per sauce**.
- Cook each chicken thigh using the same pan and heat level.
- The diced onion added to Sauce A should match the onion concentration of the onion meads (6 lb per gallon) based on the amount of mead used.

Weighing both the mead and the onion ensures the control sauce contains roughly the same onion load that exists in the onion meads.

---

## Cooking Procedure (Repeat for Each Sauce)

### 1. Cook Chicken

1. Heat skillet over medium heat and add oil to cover the bottom of the pan.
2. Place one chicken thigh in the pan.
3. Cook **6–8 minutes** until well browned.
4. Flip and cook another **4–6 minutes** until done.
5. Remove chicken from the pan and set aside, leaving the fond, and dabbing excess oil with a paper towel.

### 2. Build Sauce

#### Sauce A – Traditional Mead (Control)

1. Add the **72 g diced onion** to the pan with the fond.
2. Cook **2–3 minutes** until softened and lightly browned.
3. Deglaze with **100 g traditional mead** and reduce for **2 minutes**.
4. Add **1 demi‑glace cube**.
5. Simmer and reduce for **2 minutes** until sauce thickens.
6. Add **10 g cold butter** and swirl or whisk until fully emulsified.
7. Strain sauce through a fine mesh strainer to remove onion pieces.

#### Sauce B – Onion Mead

1. Deglaze the pan with **100 g onion mead** and reduce for **2 minutes**.
2. Add **1 demi‑glace cube**.
3. Simmer and reduce for **2 minutes** until sauce thickens.
4. Add **10 g cold butter** and swirl or whisk until fully emulsified.

#### Sauce C – Caramelized Onion Mead

1. Deglaze the pan with **100 g caramelized onion mead** and reduce for **2 minutes**.
2. Add **1 demi‑glace cube**.
3. Simmer and reduce for **2 minutes** until sauce thickens.
4. Add **10 g cold butter** and swirl or whisk until fully emulsified.

### Additional Observations

All three of these sauces could use some acid. I was hoping the mead would provide enough, but some whole grain mustard is likely the move here.

I don't think either of the onion meads are significantly better, but the flavor of the fermented onion is significantly different than fresh onion. I personally do not like the aftertaste at all. The best way I think to describe it musky, like a damp basement smells. Its subtle, but its there in both of the onion meads. Beyond that, the flavor of the two onion sauces is really good.

I think the level of effort required to makes these is too high to justify making these, but an onion mead made with a smaller quantity of onion _may_ be worth the uniqueness of flavor here **if** and **only if** that aftertaste isn't present. I don't think the caramalized onion mead is worth it in any capacity unfortunatly. I think the burnt taste might age out, its similar to what I get from a lot of bochets, but I think you're better off using a plain bochet here.

Another really important thing is that even though these do taste pretty different on a spoon side by side, they're not different enough when paired with chicken to notice anything other than "this is a good sauce." And the chicken was only seasoned with salt. I just don't think this particular cooking mead is worth the effort in a practical setting, but I am surprised at how good the two onion sauces were.
