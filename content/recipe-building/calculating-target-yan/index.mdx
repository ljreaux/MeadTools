# Calculating Target YAN

Target YAN is one of the main inputs MeadTools uses to estimate nutrient needs for fermentation.

This page explains what YAN is, how MeadTools estimates a target, and why that estimate depends on both sugar level and yeast choice.

## What YAN means

**YAN** stands for **Yeast Assimilable Nitrogen**.

This is the nitrogen in a must that yeast can actually use during fermentation.

Honey is naturally low in YAN, which is why mead often needs nutrient additions even when beer or grape wine might not.

## Why target YAN is only an estimate

There is no single universal YAN number that works for every mead.

The nitrogen requirement depends on things like:

- how much sugar is present
- which yeast strain is being used
- how much nitrogen is already present from fruit or juice

So the goal is not to discover one perfect number. The goal is to estimate a reasonable **target YAN** for the must being built.

## Step 1: Estimate sugar in grams per liter

The first step is to estimate how much sugar is present in the must.

This uses:

```txt
Sugar (g/L) = Brix × Specific Gravity × 10
```

Why this works:

- Brix describes grams of sugar per 100 grams of solution
- multiplying by 10 scales that to grams per 1000 grams
- multiplying by specific gravity adjusts from weight-based concentration to grams per liter of must

### Example

Suppose the must has:

```txt
SG = 1.108
Brix = 25.5
```

Then:

```txt
Sugar (g/L) = 25.5 × 1.108 × 10
             ≈ 282.5 g/L
```

## Step 2: Apply a yeast nitrogen multiplier

Once the sugar concentration is estimated, it is multiplied by a nitrogen-demand factor based on the yeast strain.

The traditional Scott Labs style multipliers are:

```txt
Extra Low / Very Low: × 0.5 to 0.75
Low:                  × 0.75
Medium:               × 0.90
High:                 × 1.25
```

In the MeadTools model, these are treated as:

```txt
Very Low:  × 0.75
Low:       × 0.75
Medium:    × 0.90
High:      × 1.25
Very High: × 1.80
```

The first four categories follow the common Scott Labs style framework used in modern meadmaking calculators.

The **Very High** category is an extra MeadTools category used mainly for especially demanding fermentations, such as many kveik-based nutrient plans.

## Step 3: Calculate target YAN

Once sugar concentration and yeast multiplier are known:

```txt
Target YAN = Sugar (g/L) × yeast multiplier
```

Using the previous example with a low-nitrogen yeast:

```txt
Target YAN = 282.5 × 0.75
           ≈ 211.9 ppm
           ≈ 212 ppm
```

This is the estimated total YAN target before accounting for nitrogen already present in the must.

## Step 4: Estimate the offset

Not every batch starts at zero.

Fruit, juice, or other ingredients may already contribute some usable nitrogen. If that nitrogen is known or estimated, it can be subtracted as an **offset**.

In MeadTools, the default offset is a conservative fruit-based estimate:

```txt
Offset (ppm) = (primary fruit weight in lb × 25) / primary volume in gal
```

This means:

- only **primary** fruit is counted
- the estimate is based on **25 ppm per pound of fruit per gallon**
- the result is scaled to the primary batch volume

### Example

Suppose a recipe has:

```txt
4 lb fruit
2 gal primary volume
```

Then:

```txt
Offset = (4 × 25) / 2
       = 50 ppm
```

## Step 5: Subtract the offset from the target

Once the offset has been estimated:

```txt
Final target YAN = calculated target YAN - offset
```

So if the yeast target is 212 ppm, and the estimated offset is 50 ppm:

```txt
Final target YAN = 212 - 50
                 = 162 ppm
```

This prevents overfeeding the fermentation when part of the nitrogen requirement is already being covered by the ingredients.

## Why the offset is only an estimate

The MeadTools offset is intentionally conservative and based on general meadmaking wisdom rather than hard universal fruit-YAN numbers.

That is important because:

- fruit nitrogen content varies widely
- ripeness matters
- variety matters
- processing method matters
- published YAN data is not available for many ingredients

So the offset should be understood as a practical planning estimate, not as a guaranteed measurement of the must.

In other words, MeadTools uses the offset to avoid assuming fruit contributes nothing, while still being careful not to over-credit the fruit.

## Where yeast requirement data comes from

MeadTools stores a large yeast database covering strains from multiple brands.

The nitrogen requirement category for a strain is taken from the information published by that yeast's manufacturer whenever that information is available.

That means MeadTools is not inventing nitrogen categories from scratch. It is using producer-supplied guidance and then applying the target YAN model on top of it.

Because manufacturers do not always describe nitrogen demand in exactly the same way, these categories should still be treated as practical working labels rather than absolute scientific values.

## Summary

Target YAN is estimated by:

1. converting gravity and Brix into sugar grams per liter
2. multiplying by a yeast nitrogen requirement factor
3. estimating an offset from primary fruit contribution
4. subtracting that offset from the target

In short:

```txt
Target YAN = (Brix × SG × 10 × yeast multiplier) - offset
```

This gives MeadTools a practical way to estimate how much nitrogen a must may need before converting that target into specific nutrient additions.
