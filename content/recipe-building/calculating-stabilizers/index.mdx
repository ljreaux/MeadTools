# Calculating Stabilizers

MeadTools can estimate chemical stabilization amounts for finished mead using:

- metabisulfite
- sorbate
- Campden tablet equivalents

This page explains the math behind those estimates and the conservative assumptions MeadTools makes when secondary ingredients are involved.

## What stabilizers are trying to do

Chemical stabilization usually means using **metabisulfite** together with **sorbate**.

In practical terms:

- metabisulfite helps protect against oxidation and suppresses yeast and microbes
- sorbate helps prevent surviving yeast cells from reproducing

These are usually used after fermentation is truly finished, not as a reliable way to stop an active healthy fermentation.

## MeadTools uses conservative final-batch values

One important MeadTools-specific choice is that stabilization math is based on the **final batch as it actually exists after secondary additions**.

That means MeadTools uses:

- **total final volume**, not just primary volume
- **final ABV after dilution**, not just primary ABV

This matters because secondary ingredients can:

- increase the total volume
- dilute the alcohol
- change the real amount of stabilizer needed

So MeadTools intentionally sizes stabilizers against the full finished batch rather than the smaller pre-backsweetening batch.

## Sorbate calculation

The sorbate side of the calculator is based on the stabilization guidance summarized in the Modern Meadmaking wiki, including the extrapolated high-ABV behavior.

MeadTools uses this formula:

```txt
Sorbate grams = ((-25 × ABV + 400) / 750) × volume in liters
```

Where:

- **ABV** is the final alcohol percentage
- **volume in liters** is the total finished batch volume

This is equivalent to first estimating the required potassium sorbate concentration in mg/L and then scaling it to the total batch size.

### Example

Suppose the finished mead is:

```txt
ABV = 12%
Volume = 19 L
```

Then:

```txt
Sorbate = ((-25 × 12 + 400) / 750) × 19
        = (100 / 750) × 19
        ≈ 2.53 g
```

## Why sorbate drops as ABV rises

In this model, sorbate need decreases as alcohol increases.

That is because alcohol itself helps make renewed fermentation less likely, which reduces how much sorbate is needed.

This is also why potassium sorbate becomes less important at very high ABV, and may be of limited value once a mead is already around 14-15% ABV or above.

## Sulfite calculation

The sulfite side starts with a target **free SO2** amount based on pH.

MeadTools uses the following pH-to-ppm mapping:

| pH           | Target free SO2 |
| ------------ | --------------- |
| 2.9 or lower | 11 ppm          |
| 3.0          | 13 ppm          |
| 3.1          | 16 ppm          |
| 3.2          | 21 ppm          |
| 3.3          | 26 ppm          |
| 3.4          | 32 ppm          |
| 3.5          | 39 ppm          |
| 3.6          | 50 ppm          |
| 3.7          | 63 ppm          |
| 3.8          | 98 ppm          |
| 3.9 or above | 123 ppm         |

These values are based on the free SO2 dosing guidance summarized in the stabilization article.

## Converting free SO2 target into metabisulfite grams

Once the target free SO2 ppm is chosen, MeadTools converts that into grams of metabisulfite.

For potassium metabisulfite:

```txt
K-meta grams = (liters × target ppm) / 570
```

For sodium metabisulfite:

```txt
Na-meta grams = (liters × target ppm) / 674
```

These divisors reflect how much free SO2 each form contributes by weight.

Another way to think about the same math is:

- potassium metabisulfite is treated as about **57% available SO2**
- sodium metabisulfite is treated as about **67.4% available SO2**

So the calculation is effectively:

```txt
grams needed = (liters × target ppm) / (SO2 fraction × 1000)
```

For potassium metabisulfite:

```txt
grams needed = (liters × target ppm) / (0.57 × 1000)
             = (liters × target ppm) / 570
```

For sodium metabisulfite:

```txt
grams needed = (liters × target ppm) / (0.674 × 1000)
             = (liters × target ppm) / 674
```

### Example

Suppose the finished batch is:

```txt
Volume = 19 L
pH = 3.5
```

At pH 3.5, MeadTools uses:

```txt
39 ppm
```

Then potassium metabisulfite would be:

```txt
K-meta = (19 × 39) / 570
       ≈ 1.30 g
```

## Campden tablet equivalent

MeadTools also estimates a Campden-tablet equivalent from the same pH target.

It uses:

```txt
Campden tablets = (target ppm / 75) × gallons
```

This is based on the common rule of thumb that:

```txt
1 Campden tablet in 1 gallon ≈ 75 ppm
```

Campden tablets can vary noticeably from one product to another, so MeadTools treats this as a rough tablet equivalent rather than an exact dosing standard.

### Example

If the batch is:

```txt
5 gal
pH 3.5 → 39 ppm
```

Then:

```txt
Campden tablets = (39 / 75) × 5
                ≈ 2.6 tablets
```

## Default pH behavior

If no pH reading is taken, MeadTools defaults to:

```txt
pH 3.6
```

which corresponds to:

```txt
50 ppm free SO2
```

This is a practical default, not a claim that every finished mead is actually at pH 3.6.

## Why MeadTools uses the finished total volume

This is one of the easiest places to under-dose by accident.

For example, imagine a mead that:

- ferments as 1 gallon
- is then backsweetened or fruited up to 1.2 gallons

If stabilizers were calculated only from the original 1 gallon, the final treated concentration would be too low.

MeadTools avoids that by calculating sulfite, sorbate, and Campden equivalents from the **full finished volume**.

## Why MeadTools uses the final diluted ABV

Secondary additions can also reduce ABV by dilution.

That matters because sorbate recommendations depend on alcohol content.

So MeadTools calculates sorbate using the **final ABV after secondary additions**, not the stronger primary-fermentation ABV.

This is another intentionally conservative choice.

## Important caveats

These calculations are useful planning tools, but they do not guarantee stability on their own.

They assume:

- fermentation has actually stopped
- the mead is being stabilized after fermentation, not during vigorous fermentation
- sorbate and sulfite are being used appropriately together
- pH has been measured accurately, or a reasonable default has been chosen
- stabilizers are replaced regularly and appropriately

They also do not replace good process decisions like confirming stable gravity readings before packaging.

## Summary

MeadTools estimates stabilizers by:

1. using final total batch volume
2. using final ABV after secondary additions
3. estimating sorbate from ABV
4. estimating target free SO2 from pH
5. converting that SO2 target into metabisulfite grams and Campden-tablet equivalents

In short:

```txt
Sorbate depends mostly on final ABV and total volume
Sulfite depends mostly on final pH and total volume
```

This gives MeadTools a practical and conservative stabilization estimate for finished mead, especially when backsweetening or other secondary additions have changed the final batch.
