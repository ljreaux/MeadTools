# Secondary Ingredients and Dilution

In MeadTools, ingredients can be marked as **secondary**. These are ingredients that are added after fermentation rather than being fermented themselves.

This page explains how secondary ingredients affect volume, gravity, and alcohol calculations.

---

## What “secondary” means

A secondary ingredient:

- is **not fermented**
- is added after primary fermentation
- still contributes **volume** and **density (SG)** to the final mixture

**Important:** In real brewing, secondary additions that contain sugar will ferment unless the mead has been stabilized (for example, using sulfite and sorbate) or fermentation conditions otherwise prevent yeast activity. MeadTools treats secondary ingredients as non-fermenting for modeling purposes, but this assumption depends on proper stabilization in practice.

Even though these ingredients are not fermented, they are still modeled the same way:

```txt
Brix → specific gravity → volume contribution
```

---

## Primary vs total volume

MeadTools distinguishes between two important volumes:

```txt
Primary volume = volume of fermenting ingredients
Total volume   = primary volume + secondary additions
```

Primary volume determines fermentation behavior.

Total volume determines the final composition of the mixture.

---

## How secondary ingredients affect volume

Secondary ingredients increase total volume without increasing the amount of alcohol produced.

```txt
total volume > primary volume
```

This has a direct impact on both:

- final gravity
- alcohol by volume (ABV)

---

## Dilution and ABV

Alcohol is produced during primary fermentation.

Once fermentation is complete, adding more liquid increases volume but does not increase alcohol.

Conceptually:

```txt
ABV = alcohol produced / total volume
```

So when total volume increases:

```txt
ABV decreases
```

This is known as **dilution**.

---

## Secondary ingredients and final gravity

Even though secondary ingredients are not fermented, they still have a **specific gravity**.

When they are added, they are blended into the existing mixture.

Conceptually:

```txt
Final SG = blend(FG_primary, SG_secondary)
```

Using the same blending equation:

```txt
Final SG = (finished mead volume × finished mead SG
          + secondary addition volume × secondary addition SG
          + any other added volume × its SG) / total volume
```

This means:

- secondary ingredients can raise the final gravity
- higher Brix secondary additions result in higher final gravity

---

## Example: backsweetening with honey

Start with a finished traditional mead:

```txt
3 lb honey
Water to 1 gallon total volume
```

From the previous calculations:

```txt
Primary volume = 3.785411784 L (1 gallon)
OG ≈ 1.10543
FG ≈ 0.996
```

This produces alcohol during fermentation:

```txt
ABV_primary ≈ 14.53%
```

### Step 1: Add secondary honey

Now add 1 lb of honey (79.60 °Bx) after fermentation.

```txt
1 lb = 0.45359237 kg
SG ≈ 1.41494

Volume_L = 0.45359237 / 1.41494 ≈ 0.32057 L
Volume_gal ≈ 0.08469 gal
```

### Step 2: New total volume

```txt
Primary volume = 3.78541 L
Added honey volume = 0.32057 L

Total volume = 4.10599 L
```

### Step 3: Dilution of alcohol

The added honey does not contribute alcohol, so the existing alcohol is diluted by the new total volume.

```txt
ABV_final = ABV_primary × (primary volume / total volume)

ABV_final ≈ 14.53 × (3.78541 / 4.10599)
           ≈ 13.40%
```

### Step 4: Final gravity after backsweetening

The honey also raises the final gravity by blending with the finished mead.

```txt
Backsweetened FG =
((primary volume × FG) + (honey volume × SG_honey)) / total volume

≈ ((3.78541 × 0.996) + (0.32057 × 1.41494)) / 4.10599
≈ 1.02871
```

### Result

After backsweetening:

- Total volume increases
- ABV decreases due to dilution
- Final gravity increases due to added sugar

This reflects the real behavior of stabilized backsweetening, where sugar is added without additional fermentation.

---

## Why this matters

Secondary additions are common in real recipes:

- backsweetening with honey
- adding fruit after fermentation
- flavoring additions

Because these additions change both **volume** and **density**, they cannot be modeled by simple adjustments.

MeadTools accounts for this by:

- treating secondary ingredients as additional solution components
- blending them into the final mixture
- recalculating both gravity and ABV

---

## Summary

Secondary ingredients:

- do not contribute to fermentation
- increase total volume
- dilute alcohol content
- raise final gravity based on their sugar concentration

This allows MeadTools to model real-world recipe adjustments more accurately than approaches that ignore volume or density changes.
